austincodingacademy.com - static site with AMP JavaScript

Link to Austin Coding Academy

austincodingacademy.com is the marketing site and student portal for Austin Coding Academy, a local coding bootcamp. It is a static site written with Jekyll, with content populated by forestry.io, and utilizing Google's AMP JavaScript library.

Charles built the first version of the currently live site in December 2018, and continued to develop and maintain the site as the school grew and changed until May, 2019.

Developing this site was an exciting challenge for Charles because he was completely new to Google's webmaster tools and AMP (formerly Accelerated Mobile Pages, a streamlined and SEO-focused JavaScript library). Working within the framework of Google's guidelines for SEO, as well as overall minimizing the size and scripting of the site, meant that the most obvious approaches to goals were not always the most appropriate.

SEO and AMP

It was, and likely remains, very important for Austin Coding Academy to improve SEO. As a small, local school with relatively little advertsing budget, being discovered in searches is an efficient way to bring in new students.

One element of SEO is copy, and I had little influence over that. Our marketing specialist was far better equipped to make those decisions when they mattered for SEO. Another factor is HTML structure. In general, it's not difficult to do okay here - don't try to be sneaky, use logical text hierarchies, and understand your semantic elements. There were probably a few unintuitive techniques a specialist would have known about, but we consistently did well in our search placement, so it was not a concern to squeeze out a little more.

The final and most concrete factor in SEO was Google's webmaster tools. I had full access to these and checekd them regularly. Every time Google crawled our site it would have new recommendations or concerns. One of the guidelines of Google's SEO policy is to favor sites with predictable interactions - specifically, this means using Google's AMP JavaScript library and not including any custom scripts.

It was actually very fun to be constrained to AMP's library of specific components. It meant giving up a lot of capabilities, but the site was initially built completely free of JS in preparation for the addition of AMP, so there wasn't anything I had to actually remove. While I didn't want to build anything too complex, as I wanted to site to work well even with JS disabled, even relatively simple interactions could be interesting challenges.

AMP has a component called the amp-position-observer that can track the user's scroll position on the page. I wanted to use this for two things: a sticky global nav bar that would fade in and out, and a floating nav menu on our longer pages. Both of these were things that other sites were doing with various levels of success, but since we were using AMP instead of custom JS it wasn't a guarantee we could do better.

The sticky global nav seemed easy at first. There are a lot of examples of such things around the internet, and in concept it's not very difficult. But without full control of the scripts, it was a real challenge to trigger the animation with precision. We wanted to nav bar to be completely transparent at the top of the page, fading into the hero image, but the background needed to being filling in as soon as the user began to scroll down, so that the nav was readable as it passed over the text in the hero image.

As there were several interconnected pieces to the amp-animation component that was reading the amp-position-observer, and documentation around both of their parts was not the most thorough, I never found the right calculations for this balance point across all screen sizes. Given more time I would have liked to keep hunting for examples or tutorials on the specific problems I was encountering, but eventually I erred on the side of readability and had to move on to other tickts.

The latter item, the floating nav menu, was less complex in comparison. AMP doesn't allow a developer to conditionally add classes for CSS, which would often be the easiest way to move or hide an element, but as before I was able to manipulate the element's opacity based on scroll position. This was much easier, since there was a larger portion of the page on which the element could be completely transparent and hidden (I didn't want the menu to appear at all until the user had scrolled most or all of the hero image off the page). I got this working quickly, tweaked it so it was shiny and smooth, and then found we were changing the structure of our courses and the entire page was about to be redone.

The new course pages were shorter and much easier to navigate by scrolling, so there was no need for the floating nav menu. I kept all the code intact so that if we developed other long pages it could be used there, but as far as I know it never did get deployed - which is really a good thing, because it means we were displaying concise, easily navigable information.
