art review: Months in the Gregorian Calendar

Month Tier List (ranked by value of rent paid)

Note 1: For the sake of simplicity, February is considered to be 28.25 days in the following calculations. In non-leap years (that is, most of them), this actually makes February look slightly less problematic than it actually is.

Note 2: These calculations are tied to the world as it is at the time of writing (2020), which means long-term corrections in the length of a year are not relevant. A year is considered to be simply the sum of its days, which is exactly 365.25 in this article.

Calculating an Average Month

There are always several ways to calculate an "average". The most common method is the mathematical mean, which is just adding up each value and dividing by the number of items. This is useful for calculations, though it doesn't always translate as well to a relatable experience. It doesn't necessarily represent the most common value you could expect to find - this is the mathematical mode.

The mode month would be 31 days, because there are more months with 31 days in them than with any other value. But this isn't actually very useful when determining the value of a month. It undersells how good these months are by declaring them the baseline and overstates how bad other months are.

I will be ranking months according to mean calculations, which will result in a non-whole-number of days (so an "average month" will come out to be a certain number of days plus a certain number of hours). But even here there are different ways to calculate the mean, and each has a different use in different context.

  • Twelve Month Mean (TMM)

    A year, as stated above, is 365.25 days in this article. Knowing that there are twelve months, we can divide a year by 12 to find the average value of what evenly spaced months "should" be: 30.4375 days. This is useful because it provides us a baseline for how rent could be adjusted based on actual number of days.

  • Non-February Mean (NFM)

    February is an outlier, and if we're not looking at an entire year, we can ignore it for certain comparisons. Since rent is paid for a month, not for a portion of a calendar year, we can compare the other 11 months of the year by only finding the average of their 337 days. This comes out to 30.6364 days, nearly 5 hours longer than the TMM.

    Why is this useful? As I said, rent isn't paid as a portion of a year. A 6-month lease from May to October doesn't include February, so when comparing the real months involved it can be helpful to use the NFM. When simply comparing one month to another, this is the value we should use. When determining what a month is really worth knowing that the month has to exist as a twelfth of a calendar year, we should use the TMM.

  • S Tier: 31-day months

    These months offer the most time for rent paid. These months are more than 8 hours longer than the NFM - this is an entire night's sleep and then some. For this reason these months truly offer exceptional value. Luckily for us, slightly more than half of the months are 31 days.

    These months shine even more in the TMM. They offer more than half a day of time above the average expected value of a month.

    • January
    • March
    • May
    • July
    • August
    • October
    • December

  • C Tier: 30-day months

    These months are simply not a good value. Compared to the TMM, these months lose us 10.5 hours each - you could watch a movie and get a full night's sleep easily in that time. When compared to the NFM, these months are even worse: nearly two-thirds of a day are lost by paying to live through one of these months.

    B Tier months comprise one-third of the year, which, if we're going by the standard expectation of spending one-third of one's day working, is a roughly acceptible sacrifice.

    • April
    • June
    • September
    • November

  • F Tier: February

    February is a scam. While it might seem nice and neat that it's exactly four weeks long most years, that's simply not what a month represents. By being only 28.25 days long, February robs the renter of days of their time. We pay our landlords for sunrises that don't exist. We pay for movie nights where nothing is watched. We pay for an entire weekend that evaporates before we can even imagine it. I cannot ever recommend living through February.

    • February
