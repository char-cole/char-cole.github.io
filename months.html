tier list: Months in the Gregorian Calendar

The Length of a Year

There are two ways to measure a year: actual length and average length.

Actual length is simply the actual number of days in a year, which varies between common and leap years. Actual length is useful for comparing the value of a given month in a specific year with other months in that same calendar year. For exmaple, comparing the value of rent paid in October 2019 with November 2019.

Average length is useful for comparing value over an arbitrary number of years, no matter how many leap years are in it. This would be quicker for comparing the value of months over a period of 5 years, for example.

Calculating an Average

There are always several ways to calculate an "average". The most common method is the mathematical mean, which is just adding up each value and dividing by the number of items. This is useful for calculations, though it doesn't always translate as well to a relatable experience. It doesn't necessarily represent the most common value you could expect to find - this is the mathematical mode. The mode can show you, for example, exactly what the most likely actual month will be.

The mode month would be 31 days, because there are more months with 31 days in them than with any other value. But this isn't actually very useful when determining the value of a month. It undersells how good these months are by declaring them the baseline and overstates how bad other months are. By using a different measurement, we can appreciate how the good months give us more than the average month.

I will be ranking months according to mean calculations, which will result in a non-whole-number of days. But even after deciding that, there is still no one right way to calculate the mean. I will make calculations for two different means that are useful in different contexts:

  • Twelve Month Mean (TMM)

    We always divide a calendar year into twelve months, so this value is simply found by dividing the number of days in a calendar year by 12. Like the mean length of a year, this is most useful for comparing the value of an arbitrary number of months that may include any number of Februaries.

    Common Year 365 days 30.4167 days/month
    Leap Year 366 days 30.5 days/month
    Mean Year 365.25 days 30.4375 days/month

  • Non-February Mean (NFM)

    February is an outlier, and if we're not looking at an entire year, we can ignore it for certain comparisons. When we pay rent for a month, we're not actually paying for one-twelfth of the year, we're just paying until there are no more days left in the month. Therefore, when we're comparing a set of months that doesn't include a February, such as a 6-month lease starting in May, this is a useful value to use.

    Year Excluding February 337 days 30.6364 days/month

    The NFM is nearly 5 hours longer than the mean TMM. It's important to value these hours.

The Tier List

Since there are only 3 different kinds of months (31, 30, or February), several tiers are empty. For a reminder, the order of tiers from best to worst is: S A B C D F

S Tier: 31-day months

These months offer the most time for rent paid. These months are more than 8 hours longer than the NFM - this is an entire night's sleep and then some above the average non-February month.

These months shine even more against the TMM. S Tier months are exactly half a day longer than the mean month in a leap year. In a common year they clock in at just under 14 hours of above-average time.

  • January
  • March
  • May
  • July
  • August
  • October
  • December

C Tier: 30-day months

These months are simply not a good value. Compared to the TMM of a leap year, each of these months falls 12 full hours short. In a common year they're 10 hours under the average - still more than a good night's sleep lost. But against the NFM these months are each missing over 15 hours.

I don't recommend spending a month's rent on a C Tier month given the choice. They're not a disaster, but there's just no upside to losing half a day. Unfortunately these months comprise a full third of the year:

  • April
  • June
  • September
  • November

F Tier: February

February is a scam. No matter what metric you use, February robs the renter of days of their time. In the best-case scenario, a leap year, the 29-day February is 36 hours shorter than the average month - from Sunday morning to Monday night. But in common years, three out of every four, time lost to February comes out to more than 58 hours. This is long enough to take a one- or two-night trip to almost anywhere in the world near a major airport.

In February, we pay our landlords for sunrises that don't exist. We pay for movie nights where nothing is watched. We pay for an entire weekend that is forgotten before we can even imagine it. I cannot ever recommend paying rent in February.

  • February

What do we do about this?

There are many possible solutions. One is to simply prorate rate according to the actual number of days in a month. This would make February the cheapest month to rent and therefore top tier in my opinion. But another solution is to change our entire calendar.

Countless brilliant minds have worked on ways to reconcile the various motions of the Earth and its moon. I'm not smarter than all of them, and I've only been able to create this because they've done so much work before me. And creating calendars is hard because there are so many different constraints you may way to satisfy. Is it important to keep a 7-day week - or to even have weeks? Why not create a year of numbered days and do away with groupings entirely?

What I'm saying is that, once again, there's no one right answer. And it will take a lot of hard work and luck to get large populations to switch over to a new calendar, even if there are inherent benefits. In fact, the best calendar for universal use on Earth may have already been created: there have been many attempts in the past, some of which I really like, but none have overtaken the Gregorian Calendar.

So as an exercise in creation, and to prevent this article from being pointless negativity, I'll make a calendar. Understand I'm not offering this as the best possible calendar, only one that asks us to look beyond tradition and make systems that work for us. Here is an attempt at a stable, secular calendar that is fair to those who pay for the right to live through it:

An Equal Terrestrial Calendar

This calendar retains the basic structure of organizing days into weeks and months. In this calendar, unlike the Gregorian, all months are composed directly of whole weeks. There are no weeks with days that belong to two different months. There are, however, a few intercalary days. These belong to no month. They are not regular days of the week, but holidays. Leap years must still exist because the revolution of the Earth around the sun is not a whole number of days. Arbitrarily, I choose to keep their affect as small as possible.

The Five-Day Week

Weeks in this calendar are only five days long. This is a drastic change from the seven-day week, with frankly unpredictable effects on the work week and the economy. Assuming workers had the power to resist regression in the balance of work days to days off, no fewer than 2/7 of days could be weekends. One day out of five would not cut it. I would strongly advocate for two out of every five days to be weekends in the standard work week.

I believe in many office jobs the three-day work week would achieve comparable productivity to the 40-hour, five-day work week. It's also possible a more nuanced structure would evolve: perhaps the fourth day would regularly be a half-day. Perhaps there would only be one day off but each work day would be reduced to 6 hours.

The Six-Week Month

Each month would be exactly six weeks, or 30 days. This results in a few nice side effects, besides enforcing equal value of monthly payments.

One effect is that it lines up better than the Gregorian Calendar with the lunar cycle, which is a little under 30 days. It's impossible for any calendar with regular months to avoid drifting from the moon phase - but, for any two sequential months in any year, you will always be able to expect the same moon phase on about the same date.

Another effect is that the numerical date of each day in each month always falls on the same day of the week. This is arguably less fun when it comes to superstition such as Friday the 13th, but it does make calculating things much easier.

The Intercalary Days

Twelve months of six weeks each makes 360 days. We're still 5 days short of a common year. I see three options for using them as intercalary days: lump them into a holiday week, spread them out across the year, or put them in two groups.

A holiday week is easy. At the beginning of the year (which I would place around the vernal equinox), simply have an entire week, with regular weekday names, that belongs to no month. This would be a week of holidays and then the first day of the year would start at the beginning of the next week. In common years, this is very simple to calculate. In leap years we still have one more day to add in - this could be placed at the end of this holiday week, but I would probably put it in between months right around the autumnal equinox - the middle of the year.

Individual days are harder to space evenly, because twelve months aren't divisble by five. If we try to space them evenly, one day in between every other pair of months, we end up with one pair of months with no holiday. Enter the leap year: adding a sixth intercalary day means that no pair is skipped, at least every four years. In this example the year might start on an intercalary day (it's important that the first day of the new year is a universal holiday - people will be staying up late and drinking the night before either way), then move to 1 January, then progress through 30 February before having another intercalary day. I generally like this evenly spaced approach except I think people would rue the last four months of the year for having no intercalary days in common years (although national or religious holidays can of course still exist within months).

The final option is somewhere in between. An uneven split of days might put three intercalary days at the beginning of the year before January, then two days between June and July. Every four years a leap day needs to be added, and this can easily be inserted before July, making the two evenly-spaced holidays have equal length. I think this is my favorite choice because it neatly splits the year into two parts and is the least affected by the lack of a leap day. The key issue with the leap day is not going to be how much do people enjoy having it, but how much do they miss it when it's not there. With this approach, the leap day feels like a nice bonus to an already enjoyable holiday.
